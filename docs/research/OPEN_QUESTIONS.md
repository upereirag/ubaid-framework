# Open Research Questions

The UBAID Framework is published in draft because the author considers community adversarial review the only path to maturity comparable to historical analogues. Frameworks of similar ambition — Asimov's Laws, the OSI model, MITRE ATT&CK — achieved durability not because they were correct upon creation, but because they survived sustained critique from adversarial reviewers across multiple disciplines.

The following five questions invite focused critique. Each represents a specific structural vulnerability the framework may have. Each is open. The author welcomes proposed answers, proposed reframings, and proposed demonstrations that the question itself is malformed.

Contributions should be submitted via the structured contribution form (see [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../CONTRIBUTING.md)) and reference the specific question number.

---

## Question 1 — Detectability of the Entity Threshold

**Is the Entity Threshold operationally detectable, or is it a circular definition?**

The framework defines the Entity Threshold as "the point at which the totality of available human knowledge and tools is insufficient to contain or control a system." This is an operational definition based on observable outcomes: containment fails completely and reproducibly with all available instruments.

The vulnerability: this definition may be tautological. Failure of containment is itself the criterion that proves the Entity Threshold was crossed. There is no independent indicator that allows pre-failure detection. An organization may never know it is dealing with an Entity rather than an exceptionally sophisticated Autonomous AI until the response has already failed.

**Specific lines of critique invited:**

- Can the threshold be reformulated as a leading indicator rather than a lagging outcome?
- Are there formal information-theoretic or computational-complexity criteria that could distinguish Autonomous AI from Entity prospectively?
- Is the circularity a fatal structural flaw, or a feature reflecting the genuine epistemological limit of the framework?
- How does this compare to analogous threshold problems in physics (event horizons) and complex systems theory (phase transitions)?

## Question 2 — Vectorial Composition at Scale

**Does vectorial composition of negative masses collapse in cases involving more than five simultaneous legal celestial bodies?**

The framework asserts that the gravitational force returning an Astronaut to neutralization is the vector composition of forces from multiple legal celestial bodies acting simultaneously. Each legal department contributes a vector toward the observed Astronaut, and the resultant determines the response trajectory.

The vulnerability: the framework has been described conceptually but not stress-tested at scale. Real incidents may involve six, ten, or fifteen relevant response departments simultaneously — incident response, network security, forensics, legal, communications, executive leadership, external regulators, law enforcement, vendor security teams, customer support. The mathematics of vector composition becomes computationally demanding, the directionality of multiple low-mass contributors may produce destructive interference, and the framework offers no guidance on how to prevent or resolve such interference.

**Specific lines of critique invited:**

- Is there a formal upper bound on the number of legal celestial bodies after which composition becomes mathematically unstable?
- Should the framework include explicit interference resolution rules, or does the existing mass-calibration system handle this implicitly?
- Are there real or simulated cases where high-multiplicity composition has been tested?
- Does the model need to incorporate notions of phase or coherence beyond simple vector addition?

## Question 3 — Necessity of the Black Hole Category

**Is the Black Hole category structurally necessary, or is it over-engineering?**

The framework formally establishes Black Holes as celestial bodies of unknown nature — observable through their gravitational effect but not yet describable in the catalogued vocabulary. The category exists between the 14 known illegal celestial bodies and the Creative Singularity Entity that may produce new bodies.

The vulnerability: this intermediate category may be unnecessary. Unknown phenomena could be treated simply as "uncatalogued" until classification is achieved, without requiring a distinct ontological category. The Black Hole concept may be adding ontological commitment where epistemic humility would suffice.

**Specific lines of critique invited:**

- Does the Black Hole category add operational value distinct from "unclassified but observed"?
- Is the protocol for Black Hole response substantively different from the protocol for any uncatalogued phenomenon?
- Does the category create a useful research focus that would be lost in a flat "unknown" classification?
- Are there parallels in other classification systems (biological taxonomy, astronomy) that validate or invalidate this structural choice?

## Question 4 — Operationalization of the Involuntary Catman

**Can the distinction between Voluntary and Involuntary Catman be operationalized without collapsing into social determinism?**

The framework proposes that some Catman entities entered cognitive symbiosis through genuine informed choice, while others — the Involuntary Catman — had their agency compromised before the symbiosis began through structural conditions that systematically devalued the development of critical thinking capacity. The Involuntary Catman is assigned the lowest culpability within the Catman taxonomy.

The vulnerability: this distinction risks collapsing into social determinism that excuses all action by attributing it to environmental conditions. If "the system made me do it" becomes a valid culpability defense at scale, the framework may dissolve responsibility rather than calibrate it. The opposite risk also applies: ignoring structural factors may unjustly penalize entities whose agency was genuinely compromised.

**Specific lines of critique invited:**

- What concrete, observable criteria distinguish Voluntary from Involuntary Catman in a specific case?
- Can the distinction be made without requiring assessment of someone's lifetime educational and social conditions?
- Does the framework need a third category (Partially Voluntary) or a continuous scale rather than a binary?
- How does this distinction interact with existing legal doctrines of capacity, intent, and mens rea?

## Question 5 — Astronaut-as-Occurrence in Identified-Attacker Cases

**Does the "Astronaut as occurrence" taxonomy survive cases where the attacker is clearly an identifiable individual?**

The framework defines the Astronaut as an occurrence rather than an individual: an observable event, a call, an object of analysis. The category (Human, Catman, Autonomous AI) refers to the nature of origin, not to the identity of the Astronaut itself.

The vulnerability: in real incidents, the attacker is often identified — a named insider, an attributed nation-state actor, a known individual hacker. In these cases, the "occurrence" framing may obscure rather than clarify. The framework's insistence on distinguishing the observable event from its origin may be philosophically rigorous but operationally awkward when the origin is fully known.

**Specific lines of critique invited:**

- Does the occurrence framing add explanatory power, or does it complicate cases that traditional individual-actor framing handles cleanly?
- Are there incidents where the occurrence framing actively misleads investigation or response?
- Can the framework accommodate cases of identified attackers without contradicting its taxonomy?
- Is there a way to make the occurrence framing default while allowing identified-individual cases to be handled as a degenerate case where occurrence and origin collapse to the same entity?

## Submission Guidelines

Responses to these questions should:

- Reference the specific question number
- Engage with the framework's own logic rather than rejecting its premises (rejection of premises is a separate, legitimate contribution category but distinct from answering these questions)
- Propose concrete reformulations where possible, not merely identify problems
- Cite adjacent literature, formal frameworks, or real cases that bear on the question
- Be submitted via the structured contribution form linked in [CONTRIBUTING.md](../../CONTRIBUTING.md)

The author reviews submissions, incorporates accepted contributions into subsequent versions with attribution, and provides written rationale for rejected contributions. All contributors are listed in framework acknowledgments.
